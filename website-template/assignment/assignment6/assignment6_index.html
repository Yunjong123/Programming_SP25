Author’s Perspective

Want to feel better, have more energy and even add years to your life? Just exercise.

The health benefits of regular exercise and physical activity are hard to ignore. Everyone benefits from exercise, no matter their age, sex or physical ability.

Need more convincing to get moving? Check out these seven ways that exercise can lead to a happier, healthier you.

1. Exercise controls weight
Exercise can help prevent excess weight gain or help you keep off lost weight. When you take part in physical activity, you burn calories. The more intense the activity, the more calories you burn.

Regular trips to the gym are great, but don't worry if you can't find a large chunk of time to exercise every day. Any amount of activity is better than none. To gain the benefits of exercise, just get more active throughout your day. For example, take the stairs instead of the elevator or rev up your household chores. Consistency is key.

2. Exercise combats health conditions and diseases
Worried about heart disease? Hoping to prevent high blood pressure? No matter what your current weight is, being active boosts HDL ("good") cholesterol and decreases unhealthy triglycerides, lowering your risk of cardiovascular diseases.

Regular exercise helps prevent or manage many health problems and concerns, including stroke, metabolic syndrome, high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, depression, anxiety, many types of cancer, arthritis, and falls. It also improves cognitive function and lowers the risk of death from all causes.

3. Exercise improves mood
Need an emotional lift? Or need to lower stress after a stressful day? A gym session or brisk walk can help. Physical activity stimulates brain chemicals that may leave you feeling happier, more relaxed, and less anxious.

You may also feel better about your appearance and yourself when you exercise regularly, which boosts your confidence and self-esteem.

4. Exercise boosts energy
Winded by grocery shopping or household chores? Regular physical activity can improve your muscle strength and endurance.

Exercise sends oxygen and nutrients to your tissues and helps your cardiovascular system work more efficiently. Improved heart and lung health gives you more energy to tackle daily chores.

5. Exercise promotes better sleep
Struggling to snooze? Regular physical activity can help you fall asleep faster, get better sleep, and deepen your sleep. Just don't exercise too close to bedtime.

6. Exercise puts the spark back into your sex life
Do you feel too tired or out of shape to enjoy intimacy? Exercise can improve energy levels and body confidence, which may boost your sex life.

It may also enhance arousal for women, and men who exercise regularly are less likely to have problems with erectile dysfunction.

7. Exercise can be fun — and social!
Exercise gives you a chance to unwind, enjoy the outdoors, or do activities that make you happy. It also helps you connect with family or friends in a fun social setting.

So take a dance class, hit the hiking trails, or join a soccer team. Find a physical activity you enjoy — and just do it!

Exercise to feel better and have fun
The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services recommends:

  • At least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week, or 75 minutes of vigorous activity.
  • Strength training for all major muscle groups at least twice per week.
Activities include brisk walking, biking, swimming, running, heavy yardwork, dancing, and using resistance bands or body weight for strength training.

Even short bursts of activity throughout the day can offer benefits. If you have health concerns, check with a professional before starting a new program.

— Mayo Clinic Staff, 7 Benefits of Regular Physical Activity

My Perspective

While the article presents a wide range of benefits associated with regular physical activity, it’s important to acknowledge that exercise is not universally beneficial for everyone in every circumstance. For individuals with certain medical conditions, chronic fatigue, or physical disabilities, exercise can sometimes lead to increased pain, fatigue, or even injury. Furthermore, the growing pressure in modern society to maintain a fit and active lifestyle can contribute to feelings of inadequacy or guilt in those who are unable to meet these expectations. Excessive or improperly supervised exercise routines, especially without sufficient rest or nutrition, can lead to problems such as joint damage, hormonal imbalance, weakened immune function, and mental burnout. In these cases, exercise may do more harm than good.

Additionally, the glorification of exercise often overlooks the fact that not everyone has access to the time, space, or resources to maintain a consistent workout regimen. People working multiple jobs, dealing with mental health struggles, or living in environments lacking safe recreational space may find it difficult or even impossible to follow these guidelines. It's also worth mentioning that intense or high-impact exercise may not be suitable for older adults or people recovering from illness. Therefore, while exercise does provide benefits for many, we must be careful not to generalize its effectiveness or push a one-size-fits-all approach. Health and well-being are multifaceted, and for some, rest, therapy, nutrition, or low-impact alternatives may offer more appropriate and sustainable paths to wellness.
